To Gate or Not to Gate: A 280-Run Study of Validated Skill Optimization

SkillOpt trains an agent's skill document the way you train weights — and its signature safety mechanism is a validation gate: no edit ships unless it survives a held-out check. But is the gate actually necessary? What should it measure? Should the epoch-end consolidation pass the same bar? We answer with a 280+-run controlled study — then ship the answers as SkillOpt-Sleep, a nightly self-evolution plugin for Claude Code, Codex, and Copilot.

Study design

SkillOpt's loop: a frozen target agent rolls out tasks; an optimizer model proposes bounded add/delete/replace edits to one skill document; each candidate is scored on a held-out validation set drawn from the same distribution as training. The gating policy decides what to do with that score. We compare five policies, identical in every other respect:

PolicyWhat it does with the validation score
No-GateNothing — every candidate is accepted. (Validation is still measured, enabling post-hoc analysis.)
Hard-GateAccept only if mean exact-correctness on validation does not drop.
Soft-GateAccept on the partial-credit score (token-F1 / per-case progress).
Mixed-GateAccept on the average of hard and soft.
Hard-Gate + Validated ConsolidationHard-Gate, and the epoch-end slow update must also win validation selection instead of being force-injected (Part 3).

Each policy runs under 4 optimization variants (step-size × schedule) on three benchmarks with execution-grade scoring — SearchQA (1,400-item held-out test, SQuAD exact match), LiveMathematicianBench (124 frontier-math MCQs), and SpreadsheetBench (280 cases where the generated openpyxl program is executed and the produced workbook is compared cell-by-cell against a golden one) — across GPT-5.5, GPT-5.4-mini, and GPT-5.4-nano, with GPT-5.5 as the optimizer throughout.

How we keep comparisons fair. All cross-policy numbers below average over the identical set of (model × variant) runs — the three GPT targets present in every arm (n=12 per policy per benchmark) — so no policy's mean is shifted by containing a different mix of models. A local Qwen3.5-4B ran in a subset of arms and is reported only as separate, per-cell evidence. Single-seed runs: we treat differences under ~1.5 points as noise, label directional results as such, and link every table to its raw source.
Part 1

Do you need the gate at all?

The surprising answer: on average, the gate neither buys nor costs accuracy. Its real product is the worst case — and how bad the worst case can get depends on how trustworthy the validation signal is.

Average effect: a wash

BenchmarkNo-GateHard-GateSoft-GateMixed-Gate
SearchQA+11.3+11.1+11.2+11.0
LiveMathematicianBench+7.0+6.7n/a — MCQ has no partial credit
SpreadsheetBench+23.2+24.9+24.0+26.0

Net lift of the final skill over baseline, in points; mean over the identical 12 (model × variant) runs per cell. SpreadsheetBench's Mixed-Gate edge (+2.8 over No-Gate) is directional — consistent with "gate on the densest signal the task provides" — but within single-seed noise; we do not claim it as established.

Finding 1 — On a clean optimization signal, the gate is redundant for the mean. On SearchQA all four policies land within 0.3 points of each other. When nearly every proposed edit is genuinely good, a filter has nothing to filter. If all you care about is average accuracy on an easy-to-optimize task, you could drop the gate — see Part 2 for the cheaper alternative that this enables.

Worst case: the gate is only as good as its validation signal

The averages hide the interesting cell. On LiveMath — a noisy task scored 0/1 with no partial credit, validated on only 18 held-out items — the per-model picture splits:

LiveMath, mean final liftNo-GateHard-Gate
GPT-5.5+5.0+6.5
GPT-5.4-mini+8.9+9.7
GPT-5.4-nano+7.0+3.0

n=4 variants per cell (3 for nano). On nano, 2 of 3 gated runs selected a "best" skill that scores below baseline on test.

Finding 2 — A gate fed by a noisy validation signal can mis-select. For the weakest model on the noisiest task, the 18-item validation set is loud enough to pass edits that don't generalize: the gated runs trail the ungated ones, and two of three pick a below-baseline "best". The principle that survives every experiment in this study: the gate's value equals the quality of its validation signal — size it accordingly, and gate on the densest score the task provides.

So is the gate removable? At research scale, often yes — for the mean. What the averages cannot show is the unbounded downside of ungated self-evolution when the proposal stream turns adversarial. We return to exactly that, with a controlled stress test, in Part 4.

Part 2

Selecting the best skill with a validation set

If the gate's mean effect is a wash, the validation set's real job is selection: out of every skill the run produced, which one do you ship? Three evidence-backed rules — plus the one intervention that reliably improves the pool you select from.

Rule 1 — You can select after training instead of gating during it

Because our No-Gate arm still measures validation at every step, we can compare in-training gating against post-hoc selection over the ungated trajectory — same budget, no rejections. The post-hoc winner matches the gated runs within noise on every benchmark (validation-selected skill, mean lift: SearchQA +11.0 vs +10.8; SpreadsheetBench +24.9 vs +24.1). For batch training pipelines, gate-free + select-afterwards is a legitimate, simpler design — provided nothing consumes the intermediate skills.

Rule 2 — Don't over-trust the validation ranking

Is the validation-best checkpoint actually the test-best? Across the fair subset, the final skill vs the validation-chosen best differ by at most ~1.4 points in either direction (e.g. Hard-Gate: final beats its own validation-best by +1.4 on average, winning 20/35 with 8 ties; Soft-Gate: −1.2 the other way). Validation ranks approximately. Treat it as a guardrail against regressions, not an oracle for picking winners — and don't burn budget chasing per-step rankings finer than its noise floor.

Rule 3 — The biggest selection win is a better candidate pool: skill-aware reflection

Selection can only choose among what reflection proposes. Standard reflection treats every failure as proof the skill is wrong — so it edits, and sometimes destroys, rules that were right all along but simply ignored at execution time. Skill-aware reflection (SAR) makes the optimizer triage first: SKILL_DEFECT (rule is wrong → edit the body) vs EXECUTION_LAPSE (rule is fine → protect it; reinforce compliance in a guarded appendix). Paired against identically-configured baselines on GPT-5.5:

SAR routingPaired runsMean Δ (validation-selected lift)Wins
Successes + failures19+0.3 pts (neutral)10/19
Failures only22+1.6 pts13/22
Finding 3 — Triage failures; leave successes alone. Routing only failures through the defect/lapse split adds +1.6 points on top of an already-optimized baseline — the largest reliable single-knob improvement we measured, and it produced the campaign's best SearchQA result (GPT-5.5, 0.776 → 0.876). The mechanism is conservation: most of SAR's value is the valid rules it doesn't delete.
Part 3

Should the slow update pass the same bar?

SkillOpt's slow update consolidates each epoch's lessons into the skill. By default it is force-injected — it skips the gate. That asymmetry bothered us: everything else must earn its place; why not the consolidation?

So we ran the fifth policy: identical to Hard-Gate, except the slow update must also win validation selection. Paired run-for-run against force-injection:

Force-injected (default)Validation-selected
Mean net lift (34 paired runs)baseline−0.0 pts — indistinguishable
Runs where final skill = best skill8 / 3531 / 35
Gating the slow update buys determinism, not points. Accuracy is a wash — but with validated consolidation, the artifact sitting in your output directory at the end of training is the artifact that validated best, in 31 of 35 runs (vs 8 of 35 with force-injection). "The best checkpoint was actually three steps ago" stops being a failure mode. For any system that deploys its final skill automatically — including the nightly plugin below — that property matters more than a fraction of a point.
Part 4

SkillOpt-Sleep: the answers, shipped as a nightly plugin 😴

The study's conclusions are only useful if they survive contact with deployment — where there is no curated training set, no 40-step budget, and nobody watching. SkillOpt-Sleep packages the whole recipe as an open-source plugin for Claude Code, Codex, and GitHub Copilot: while you sleep, your idle API quota runs a five-stage offline cycle on your own usage history.

1HarvestCollect recent session transcripts from your local agent.
2MineDistill the recurring tasks you actually ask for.
3DreamReplay each task in multi-rollout groups + synthetic variants.
4ConsolidateDistill bounded edits from the good-vs-bad contrast (failure-focused, Part 2).
5GateShip only what passes the held-out gate (Part 1), with validated consolidation (Part 3).

Every default follows the study: the gate is on, sized to the validation signal it can actually get; consolidation is validated, so the skill in your agent's memory is always the one that measured best; reflection is failure-focused. The output is a compact, human-readable skill block in CLAUDE.md / SKILL.md — reviewable, revertible, zero inference-time overhead, fully decoupled from the research codebase.

Deployment-scale validation: the complete grid

We ran the study's protocol at deployment scale — 5 nights, only 10 new real tasks per night, dream rollout groups, associative recall — against the same full test sets and the same execution-grade evaluators, for every combination of three benchmarks × three targets × both modes. End-to-end with real agents, on the public gbrain-evals skillopt-v1 benchmark, the plugins drive real Claude Code and real Codex through the full cycle (deficient seed skills 0.00 → 1.00 on held-out tasks in all 4 seeds). Each component behind that profile is ablated below.

Component ablation 1 — what joins the dream: experience replay

Knowledge carries across nights through the skill document; the data itself need not be replayed. Is that enough? We compare three replay policies on SearchQA (GPT-5.5, both modes): none (each night dreams only on its 10 new tasks), recall (the k=10 most similar historical tasks join the dream — associative memory), and cumulative (the full history joins every night — the anti-forgetting upper bound):

Replay policyGate-free ΔGated ΔNight-5 replay cost
none+3.9+2.00 tasks
recall k=10 (shipped)+5.1+4.410 tasks
cumulative+4.8+6.040 tasks

Replay turns the one-time jump into a climb. Under cumulative replay the gated run keeps accepting genuinely better skills as late as night 5 — 0.798 → 0.814 → 0.854 → 0.854 → 0.854 → 0.858 — the best SearchQA result of all our experiments. Recall captures most of that benefit at a quarter of the night-5 replay cost, which is why it ships as the default.

Component ablation 2 — dream diversity, and what the gate is really for

Reflection learns from the contrast between good and bad rollouts of the same task, which requires the K dream rollouts to be independent samples. Ablating that diversity — reflecting on a single sample per task — degrades the proposal stream exactly the way Part 1's risk analysis predicts, and gives the gate something real to do. Grid-level effect of the dream components (all 18 cells per configuration):

Configurationmean Δworst Δ
single-sample reflection, no replay−2.7−52.8

The −52.8 deserves a close look, because it is the strongest safety result in this post. Under the degraded condition, the gate-free optimizer distilled a plausible-but-wrong rule from a single failure ("answer with the document title string, verbatim") and the weakest model followed it off a cliff. The gated twin — same model, same nights, same candidate stream — rejected every one of those edits and held its baseline:

Baseline (GPT-5.4-nano, SearchQA)

Defense in depth, each layer measured. Diverse rollouts remove the failure at its source — the same gate-free cell is +2.7 with K=5 sampling, no gate involved. Recall lifts the grid mean and tightens the worst case. And the gate is the layer that holds when everything upstream degrades: it converted a −52.8 collapse into a flat hold. A nightly self-evolution loop is dependable not because any single mechanism is perfect, but because each layer's contribution — and each layer's failure mode — has been measured.

For users who can't hold out a validation set, the gate-free mode is additionally hardened by the feature-side guardrail — a rollout-free check that rejects any proposed rule violating the task's output contract (format, language, no clarifying questions). In our runs it flipped gate-free SpreadsheetBench from regressing to gaining on both the strongest and weakest targets. Screening candidates by skill-side features alone, without any validation rollouts, is the open direction this study points to next.

Reproducibility, naming, and limitations

Sources. Study tables: outputs/blog_1/results.md and results_sar.md in the research repo (192 + 89 rows), with the team's confidence-tiered analysis in analyse_results.md. Plugin runs: raw per-run JSON under docs/sleep/blog_runs/, with tables regenerated deterministically by gen_blog.py / gen_nightly_blog.py. Every aggregate in this post is recomputed by script from those files.

Naming map (this post ↔ repo run prefixes): No-Gate = c1, Hard-Gate = c2, Soft-Gate = c3, Mixed-Gate = c4, Hard-Gate + Validated Consolidation = c5; skill-aware reflection runs carry the _sar / _sar_fo suffix.

Limitations, stated plainly. Single seed per run; cells aggregate 4 optimization variants, so differences under ~1.5 points are treated as noise and labeled directional. Cross-policy comparisons use only the model set common to all policies; the local Qwen3.5-4B appears only as per-cell evidence. Soft/Mixed gating is undefined on LiveMath (no partial credit). One early finding ("the gate modulates variance") was retracted after de-pooling revealed a model-composition confound — the same fairness rule all numbers here follow.

Provenance notes. The single-sample-reflection condition in the dream-diversity ablation originated as a response-caching defect discovered during development (all K rollouts of a task resolved to one cached sample); it is retained as a controlled ablation arm because its per-run configuration is fully documented in the committed artifacts. The deployment grids run on Azure OpenAI Responses-API endpoints; per-run JSON records the exact backend, seed, and parameters for every cell.
